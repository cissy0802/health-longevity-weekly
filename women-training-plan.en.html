TRAINING PROGRAM

Women's Training Plan
女性训练计划 — 4 Weeks · Gym & Home

4 weeks · 3× per week 40 min strength + 20 min on other days · Gym / Home versions
This plan's stance: use progressive overload across four structured weeks to systematically strengthen shoulders, back, chest, and glutes/legs. Every exercise comes in a gym and a home version—at home, a dumbbell + resistance band + mat is enough. The goal isn't training to failure but clean form, steady load increases, and long-term sustainability—building tone and good posture, not a one-off thrash.

4-week progression (progressive overload)
WeekFocusSets & intensity
Week 1 · AdaptLearn the moves, find the working muscle2 sets/exercise, easy (3–4 reps left in tank)
Week 2 · BuildAdd volume3 sets, fairly easy (2–3 reps left)
Week 3 · PushAdd load / slow the lowering3 sets, challenging (1–2 reps left)
Week 4 · ConsolidateStabilize and edge up3 sets, last set near failure (form intact)
Intensity principles
• Pick the load/band: one where you complete the target reps with the last two feeling hard
• Rest between sets: 60–90 s for strength moves
• How to progress: when a week's top rep range feels easy, next week add load, a tighter band, or one set
• Tempo: lower (eccentric) ~2 s under control—no swinging; squeeze the target muscle at the top
• Breathing: exhale on exertion; don't hold your breath for long
WARM-UP
Before each 40-minute day, do a 5-minute warm-up
• March in place / jumping jacks for 1 minute to raise body temperature
• Cat-Cow × 8, hip circles × 10/side
• Band pull-apart / shoulder circles × 15
• 1–2 light/bodyweight ramp-up sets of the day's main lift (e.g., bodyweight squats before leg day)
• Leave 5 minutes after training for stretching + a few long exhales to unwind

💡 Form cues + imagery (tap a figure above for a demo)
• Hip thrust / glute bridge: drive the pelvis up to push the ceiling away, press through the heels, chin slightly tucked; at the top squeeze the glutes "like pinching a sheet of paper" for 1 s. ✗ Don't arch the low back and push the belly up.
• Goblet squat: imagine sitting back onto a low chair behind you; knees track over the toes, don't cave in; hold the dumbbell at the chest like a goblet. ✗ Heels lifting, knees caving.
• Romanian deadlift: imagine a wall 30 cm behind you and pushing your butt back to touch it; flat back, dumbbells slide down the thighs to just below the knee, feel the hamstrings lengthen. ✗ Rounding the back, turning it into a squat.
• Lunge: imagine your body riding an elevator straight up and down, back knee lightly taps, weight in the front heel. ✗ Front knee shooting past the toes, torso pitching forward.
• Band lateral walk: half-squat "crab walk," imagine the knees constantly pushing the band apart, toes forward. ✗ Swaying side to side.
• Calf raise: rise onto the toes, lift the heels as high as possible, pause 1 s, then lower slowly; hold a wall for balance. ✗ Bouncing, using momentum.

💡 Form cues + imagery (tap a figure above for a demo)
• Lat pulldown: depress the shoulders first, then pull, imagining you tuck the elbows toward your back pockets; bar to the top of the collarbone, chest up. ✗ Leaning back and swinging, shrugging.
• Bent-over row: imagine tucking the dumbbell into your back pocket, elbow along the body toward the hip; at the top squeeze the shoulder blade "like pinching a pen behind your back," flat back throughout. ✗ Shrugging, rounding, swinging.
• One-arm row: pull the elbow toward the ceiling, brace with the other hand on a chair, don't rotate the torso. ✗ Twisting the body, shrugging.
• Face pull: pull the rope/band toward the brow, hands opening out to the sides "like drawing a bow and flexing," elbows above hands. ✗ Hands below elbows, turning it into a row.
• Lateral raise: imagine lifting a bucket of water in each hand out to the sides, pinkie slightly higher (like pouring), up to shoulder height; "lift" the arm with the outer shoulder. ✗ Shrugging, swinging, raising too high.
• Reverse fly: soft fixed elbows, imagine hugging a big tree then opening the elbows out to the sides, squeeze the upper back. ✗ Using the low back to heave up.

💡 Form cues + imagery (tap a figure above for a demo)
• Bench / floor press: retract and depress the shoulder blades "into your back pockets," press the dumbbells straight up to the ceiling, then return like hugging a big tree. ✗ Caving the chest, elbows flared to a full 90° (shoulder strain).
• Incline press / incline push-up: press up and slightly overhead; brace the core "like a plank." ✗ Sagging the low back, piking the hips.
• Fly: soft fixed elbows, open and close only at the shoulder like "hugging a tree," feel the chest stretch at the bottom. ✗ Bending the elbows into a press.
• Shoulder press: squeeze glutes and core, don't lean back, press the dumbbells straight overhead, head moves slightly out of the way. ✗ Arching the back, pressing forward-diagonally.
• Lateral raise: lift a "bucket of water" in each hand out to the sides, pinkie slightly higher, up to shoulder height; lift with the outer shoulder. ✗ Shrugging, swinging, raising too high.
• Triceps pushdown / extension: keep the upper arm "welded" to the side, only the forearm moves. ✗ Upper arm swinging for momentum.

🧘 The 20 minutes on other days (rotate among three)

A · Core + Posture (2–3 rounds)
• Dead bug (10/side): lie on your back, arms pointing to the ceiling and knees stacked over the hips in "tabletop"; slowly lower the opposite arm and leg toward straight, then switch. Imagine balancing a cup of water on your belly—keep the low back glued to the floor. ✗ Low back arching off the floor, rushing.
• Bird dog (10/side): on all fours (hands under shoulders, knees under hips), extend the opposite arm and leg into one straight line, hold 1–2 s, then switch. Imagine balancing a cup of water on your back—keep it level. ✗ Twisting/sagging the low back, lifting the leg too high.
• Plank (30–45 s): forearms directly under the shoulders, head–hips–heels in one line, brace the core and squeeze the glutes. ✗ Sagging or piking the hips, holding your breath.
• Glute bridge (15): lie with knees bent, drive the hips up through the heels, squeeze the glutes 1 s at the top. ✗ Hyperextending the low back instead of using the glutes.
• Band pull-apart (15): straight arms pull the band out to the sides, pinch the shoulder blades, imagine "opening the chest." ✗ Shrugging, bending the elbows.
• Wall chin tuck (10): back of the head against the wall, draw the chin straight back into a "double chin" to activate the deep neck flexors. ✗ Tilting the head up or down instead of retracting.
Almost no rest between moves, 45 s between rounds; targets the rounded shoulders and slack core from sitting.
B · Zone 2 Cardio (20 min)
💡 What Zone 2 is: easy, sustainable cardio that builds your aerobic/fat-burning base and aids recovery.
• How to gauge intensity: "you can speak a full sentence but not sing"; effort 3–4/10; you can mostly breathe through the nose; you finish feeling "I could keep going" (about 60–70% max HR).
• Pick any: brisk / incline walk, elliptical, stationary bike, rower, swimming, easy cycling or hiking—choose what's joint-friendly and sustainable. Incline walking spares the knees and raises HR more easily.
• Duration & frequency: start at 20 min, build to 30–45; aiming for 150 min/week of moderate cardio is a great target (this Thursday session + extend or add one on the weekend).
• Progress: add duration before incline/intensity—Zone 2 is about steady and long, not breathless.
• Tips: a podcast/audiobook makes it stick; a 10–15 min brisk walk after meals helps blood sugar; the real trap is going too hard—most people turn Zone 2 into Zone 3, so keep the HR down and you'll progress faster.
C · Stretch / Yoga + Breathe (20 min)
💡 Cues (tap a figure above for a demo). Keep the whole thing about relaxation, mobility, and breathing—no intensity, especially on Friday to save energy for the weekend.
• Pec stretch (30 s ×2): forearm on a doorframe, turn the body away slightly, feel the front of the chest open. ✗ Shrugging, holding your breath.
• Neck / upper-trap stretch (30 s/side): gently tilt the head toward the shoulder, the opposite shoulder actively drops. ✗ Yanking hard, rotating the head.
• Kneeling hip-flexor stretch (30 s/side): half-kneel, tuck the pelvis under, sink the front hip, open the front of the hip (tightest from sitting). ✗ Arching the low back and pushing forward.
• Cat-cow (8 reps): on all fours, inhale to arch and look up, exhale to round and tuck—mobilize the spine segment by segment. ✗ Moving only the head, not the back.
• Thread the needle (5/side): on all fours, thread one arm under the body, shoulder to the floor, rotate the thoracic spine. ✗ Sagging the low back instead of rotating.
• Child's pose (30 s): sit hips back to the heels, fold forward with arms reaching, and add long, slow breathing (in 4 s, out 6 s) to "downshift" the week. ✗ Breath-holding, shrugging.

⚠️ Notes for women · Safety · Myths

• Don't fear "getting bulky": women's testosterone is low, so regular strength training builds tone and definition, not a surge in girth; the "lifted glutes, flat core" look comes precisely from this kind of training.
• Menstruation: scale the load or sets to how you feel that day—no need to stop; if very uncomfortable, swap the day for a 20-minute stretch + breathe session.
• Pelvic floor & breathing: exhale on exertion with heavier loads and avoid prolonged breath-holding; if you have leaking or a sense of heaviness, master "breath–pelvic-floor coordination" before adding load, and consider pelvic-floor physiotherapy.
• Pregnancy / postpartum: adjust individually under professional guidance—don't copy this plan as-is.
• Form > load: better to go lighter with clean form than to add weight and break down.
• Red flags: sharp joint pain, or numbness/weakness radiating down an arm or leg → stop and get assessed, don't push through.
• "Spot reduction" is a myth: training a area shapes and strengthens it, but fat loss comes from an overall energy deficit + diet, not one exercise.

❓ FAQ

Is strength training just 3× a week enough?
Yes. For most people, hitting each muscle group adequately plus progressive overload makes a 3-day whole-body split progress steadily; the 20-minute days for core, cardio, and recovery actually improve recovery and sustainability.
No equipment at home—will results be much worse?
Not by much. A dumbbell + band + bodyweight provide plenty of progressive overload, especially in the early weeks. The bottleneck usually appears when glutes/legs need heavier load—then go heavier on the dumbbell, switch to single-leg variations (harder), or hit the gym for big lifts when you can.
How long until I see changes?
Strength and "feeling the muscle work" come in 2–3 weeks; visible changes in posture and tone usually take 6–12 weeks and depend heavily on sleep and diet (especially adequate protein). Repeat this 4-week plan for 2–3 cycles, nudging the load up each round.
What if I can't finish in 40 minutes?
Prioritize the first 3–4 main lifts (the big move for each area), and trim the optional items (calves, triceps) or drop one set; don't skip the warm-up. Better to do less and finish than to rush everything sloppily.
